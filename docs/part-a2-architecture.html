MAOZ · Smart Search · Part A.2

Redact at the source, do the Hebrew work at index time, ship the introduction

The solution, the data flow from Salesforce to a presented result, and the reasoning behind each choice — including the ones I expect to be challenged.

THESIS

Salesforce stays the system of record and the authorization authority. Fields excluded by the approved projection do not leave it: field-level security, an explicit allow-list and derived-field dependency review enforce that boundary. Every search revalidates the viewer with Salesforce and binds its authoritative allowed-record relation before ranking; no available authority means no search. The profile index is a disposable derivative; audit and evaluation records have their own controlled retention. And the hardest requirement — matching חינוך בלתי פורמלי to תנועות נוער when the two share no word — is built as a mechanism rather than assumed as a property of the model, because Hebrew morphology defeats lexical search precisely where this system has to succeed.

02Choices & considerations

Six decisions the brief asks me to justify. For each: what I chose, what I rejected, and the strongest objection I expect — with the answer.

Environment

One repository, one database, two long-running processes, four scheduled jobs.

An importable Python package plus a small server-rendered UI, running from one compose file on a single modest VM hosted in Israel. The same package, entry points and configuration schema run on laptop and in production; production adds identity, network, backup and monitoring controls.

Rejected: Kubernetes, a workflow orchestrator, a message queue, a separate model-serving container, managed Postgres, and a JavaScript SPA — each adds a daemon with its own upgrade cadence and failure modes to serve a handful of queries per minute.

Under challenge

“You have put a legally sensitive database on a self-hosted box owned by an organisation with no platform team. Managed Postgres would be safer.”

My first answer to this was wrong, and correcting it is more useful than defending it. I had assumed Hebrew forced self-hosting, because file-backed dictionaries — stopword lists, morphological lemmatisers — must be installed on the server, and no managed provider documents a way to do that. But Hebrew stopwords can be stripped with ts_delete inside a generated column on any managed Postgres, so that argument does not hold. The narrower case is an Israel-region deployment, cost, and keeping model inference off a hosted API; the infrastructure host still sits inside the trust boundary. Upgrades use a rebuild from Salesforce, and the target design exercises that rebuild monthly. If MAOZ cannot name a person accountable for monthly patching, the recommendation flips to managed Postgres — which, now that the Hebrew objection is gone, costs us less than I first thought.

Models & AI tools

The target design permits two local checkpoints; the measured POC ships one.

The primary encoder is BAAI/bge-m3 (MIT): it has published Hebrew retrieval evidence and requires no instruction prefix, which removes a whole class of silent prefix bug. Its 8,192-token architectural limit is headroom, not a benefit this POC realises. Aspects are embedded separately, so the unit that has to fit is one aspect, and the longest aspect in the corpus tokenises to 59 tokens; an entire profile reaches about 348 characters, or 129 tokens, even if all four were concatenated. BAAI themselves recommend max_length=1024. I keep it on the list because MAOZ's real bios may be far longer than my fixtures, not because anything here exercises it. Corrected after review: an earlier version of this paragraph said 160 characters. That counted only the four narrative fields and omitted title, organisation, sector, description, affiliations and cohort, which the projection also encodes — understating the input by roughly half. The conclusion is unchanged, and the corrected figure makes it stronger. The local cross-encoder is BAAI/bge-reranker-v2-m3 (Apache-2.0), if adopted, would apply only to the small, already-authorised candidate set. Both checkpoints stay local. Because the POC machine is CPU-only, wall-clock feasibility is a launch criterion. The Part B experiment rejected the reranker for this POC: on the frozen synthetic flagship it moved the intended profile from raw dense rank 2 to rank 4. That local result does not refute reranking in general; it makes inclusion conditional on MAOZ-owned judgments.

Two translation models were removed from this design when MAOZ confirmed the system is Hebrew. They existed to make an English query retrieve a Hebrew profile. That requirement no longer holds, so the machinery goes — along with the risk of translation errors being baked into a persisted index. English still appears inside Hebrew records as organisation names, job titles and acronyms, so the system must not break on Latin script; that is now a robustness property, not a headline capability.

These names are a measured starting point, not a claim that a model card predicts MAOZ quality. In week one, BGE-M3 is compared with a smaller multilingual baseline on MAFAT's Hebrew Semantic Retrieval National Challenge set, on MAOZ's golden queries when available, and on wall-clock CPU latency. The embedding-identity hash makes the winner replaceable by a deterministic rebuild.

Rejected: hosted embedding APIs (send profile text outside the MAOZ-controlled trust boundary); very large embedding models (wrong operational tier, and leaderboard aggregates do not predict Hebrew retrieval quality); licence-encumbered models, including non-commercial licences a nonprofit that may later partner should not have to defend. Licence is a screening criterion applied to every model, checked per repository rather than per family — the first translation model I selected turned out to carry the same non-commercial licence I had just rejected an embedding model for. That model is now gone from the design for a different reason, but the screening step stays: “the Qwen licence” and “the OPUS-MT licence” are not single things, and the check has to be per checkpoint.

Under challenge

“How do you know any of this works in Hebrew?”

The Part B POC now supplies narrow evidence, not a general answer: on one frozen synthetic corpus, 8/8 acceptance probes rank first, 7/7 unrelated probes abstain, and 7/9 held-out probes — queries written against profiles with no concept-vocabulary entry, and excluded from threshold fitting — rank first. Raw BGE-M3 put the flagship target second, the tested reranker made it fourth, and a transparent staff-owned concept bridge made the configured intent first. The held-out set exists because the configured probes were measured and found to prove less than they appeared to: before the review fixes, the assembled pipeline ranked the intended profile first on only 4 of 11 held-out queries while the dense leg alone managed 9, so the machinery was subtracting from the model. The configured probes are regression checks; only the held-out number bears on generalisation, and nine authored queries is a smoke test rather than a Hebrew evaluation. I therefore put the quality burden on components I control and can measure — the golden set, versioned vocabulary, confidence gate and morphological variants — while genuine validation waits for MAOZ-owned queries. Critically, the choice is reversible: an embedding-identity hash makes a model swap deterministic; full rebuild duration is measured on the target CPU before any operational commitment. Confidence about the process, honesty about the priors.

RAG

Retrieval and evidence — deliberately not retrieval-augmented generation.

Retrieve wide, score, gate on confidence, diversify, then explain extractively: which aspect matched, which terms matched, and the sentence that carried it — highlighted in the source text, with a badge saying whether it was self-described, staff-noted or member-confirmed. No hosted or general-purpose instruction-following LLM receives profile text at any point — and with translation removed from the design, no model in the system generates text at all.

Rejected: LLM-generated match rationales, hypothetical-document query expansion, and the chunk-and-summarise document-RAG framing — which is simply the wrong shape for short structured profiles.

Under challenge

“You were asked for an AI system and you have removed the AI. A competitor will demo a chatbot; you show highlighted text.”

This is the decision most likely to read as under-delivery, so let me name that risk rather than hide it. The AI is the encoder and the cross-encoder — they are what lets a search for חינוך בלתי פורמלי surface a profile that says תנועות נוער and shares no word with it, which is the capability the brief actually names. What I removed is general-purpose, instruction-following generation in the query path, for reasons specific to MAOZ: member-authored bios are untrusted input, so putting them in a prompt is textbook indirect prompt injection; a hallucinated attribute about a real member is a data-accuracy problem, not an embarrassment; and self-explanations are documented as unfaithful to the ranking they claim to describe. I specify a constrained, still risk-bearing upgrade path — a self-hosted model inside the same trust boundary, limited to already-extracted spans and subject to output validation — rather than treating generation as safe by default.

Storage

One PostgreSQL database with pgvector. Exact search, no approximate index.

Profile rows, vectors, lexical index, audit log and evaluation labels in one transaction boundary. Half-precision vectors keep the working set small. The permission predicate rides inside the scan as row-level security rather than filtering results afterwards.

Rejected: dedicated vector engines — the best of them solve a filtered-approximate-search problem MAOZ does not have, at the price of a second daemon, a second backup regime and a reconciliation job. Documented as the escape hatch at very large growth, where migrating is a re-index rather than a rewrite.

Under challenge

“Exact search doesn't scale — and your own design multiplies row count by aspects.”

Correct — and in fact worse than stated, which I would rather surface than be caught on. In-process the scan is genuinely cheap: on the order of 3 ms at ten thousand rows and 15–20 ms at fifty thousand, measured, because the work is memory-bandwidth-bound. Those numbers do not transfer into Postgres. A half-precision 1024-dimension vector occupies 2,056 bytes, fractionally above Postgres's ~2 kB inline threshold, so every vector is pushed out-of-line and each scan pays extra fetches plus a conversion per row; third-party reports put an unindexed scan at a million rows in seconds. So the honest position is narrower than “exact scales fine”: exact is right for the proof of concept and for a staff-only index in the low tens of thousands, and at the upper bound it needs either fewer dimensions — 512 fits inline and removes the penalty outright — or an approximate index. But the reason to start exact is correctness, not speed. With a mandatory permission filter, an approximate index can silently truncate results, and pgvector still applies filters after the index scan — iterative scan is post-filtering with retry, not true pre-filtering. A missing person in a permissions context is a defect users cannot detect and therefore cannot report. Starting exact supplies the baseline against which any later approximate index can measure filtered recall.

Embeddings

Four aspects per person, one service, and length limits counted in Hebrew tokens.

A single code path may produce a vector, and it owns the complete input contract; BGE-M3 requires no query or passage prefix. Splitting a person into aspects stops one strong signal being diluted by unrelated fields — and the winning aspect is the explanation, for free. Length caps are counted in real tokens, because Hebrew tokenises expensively and character caps truncate it invisibly.

Rejected: one blob per person (loses the aspect signal and the free explanation); mean-pooling across aspects (dilutes the one that matched); dimension reduction without measurement — 512-dimensional halfvec fits inline and avoids the 1,024-dimensional TOAST penalty, but its retrieval-recall cost must be measured; a text-only content hash — the most dangerous omission available, because a model swap would then re-embed nothing and leave queries and documents in two unrelated spaces with no error raised.

Under challenge

“You designed an elaborate bilingual indexing pipeline and then deleted it. Why should I trust the parts that are left?”

Because deleting it was the system working as intended. The bilingual machinery existed to satisfy one requirement — an English query retrieving a Hebrew profile — that I had assumed rather than confirmed, and it was the first thing I asked MAOZ about. The answer removed it. That is a discovery question paying for itself, and I would rather show the design getting simpler on evidence than defend complexity I no longer need. What survives is not the leftovers, it is the part that was always load-bearing: aspect-level embedding so one strong signal is not diluted by four unrelated fields, the winning aspect doubling as the explanation, token-counted length caps because Hebrew tokenises expensively and character caps truncate it invisibly, and an embedding-identity hash so a model swap cannot silently leave queries and documents in two unrelated vector spaces. None of those depended on translation. What replaces it is Hebrew-specific work that now matters more, not less — prefix clitics, gazetteer expansion, and a lexical leg that has no stemmer to lean on.

Components

The seam is at the projection builder — and it is what makes the design survive the open questions.

Upstream of that seam, everything deals in Salesforce truth and permissions. Downstream, everything deals only in already-redacted, already-normalised text. The seam separates content processing; it does not move authorization authority. On every request the query service revalidates the viewer with Salesforce, obtains its authoritative visible-record relation, and binds that relation into the local SQL / RLS transaction before dense or lexical ranking starts. Every ranking stage is swappable behind a single entry point, so the UI, the evaluation harness and any future in-CRM panel are independent consumers of the same contract.

If Salesforce is unavailable or cannot supply the current relation with acceptable availability and latency, every real-data request fails closed. Synthetic, offline POC evaluation may continue, but member-facing search does not launch and neither a cached ACL nor a broader role is an acceptable substitute.

Rejected: a microservice decomposition (correct at scale, actively harmful without a platform team); a single-notebook proof of concept (demos well, cannot be evaluated, permissioned or maintained); and application-layer permission filtering over an unrestricted candidate set — including retrieving top-k and only then dropping forbidden rows — because relevant allowed records can disappear, while result counts, score distributions and latency remain oracles that confirm hidden records exist.

Under challenge

“This is over-engineered for a nonprofit with a few thousand records.”

Judge the operational surface, not the component count: two processes, one database, one repository, four scheduled jobs. Most components are small modules in one package — they are named separately so each decision can be challenged individually, not deployed separately. And I'll make the objection stronger than you did: a competent developer could ship one model, one document per person and a single page, and demo the flagship moment on day three. I agree — as a sequence, and that is what the staged plan below actually does. The disagreement is only about whether the target state is written down. What kills these systems is not day-one complexity; it is month-eighteen drift.

03Sequence

The architecture above is a target state, not a first delivery. The order matters more than the inventory — and the first stage can cancel the project.

Stage 0
2 weeks
Go / no-go gate

Demand baseline — no code at all

Staff log every incoming “who do you know who…” request verbatim: what was asked, who they named, how long naming took, and whether the introduction actually happened. In parallel, MAOZ staff run a counting query inside Salesforce and publish per-aspect fill rates by sector and cohort — aggregate counts only, no profile text extracted and nothing copied out of the org.

Proves: whether findability is the bottleneck at all. If staff name candidates in under a minute and introductions die downstream, the right deliverable is an intake form and a nudge — and this stage cancels the project cheaply. It also produces the golden evaluation set as a by-product.

Stage 1
2 weeks

Thin vertical slice

Scoped extraction, aspect decomposition, local embeddings, exact vector scan, a transparent concept vocabulary, confidence gate, extractive evidence, and a facets-first UI with correct bidirectional text handling. Hybrid fusion and cross-encoder reranking were both built and both cut by the Stage 2 rule below — the ablations are in Part B.

Proves: that a Hebrew query retrieves a profile sharing no word with it — חינוך בלתי פורמלי finding תנועות נוער — with visible evidence and an honest “no strong match” when it cannot. This is approximately the simple system a sceptical reviewer would have built first. It runs on synthetic profiles. Real member data waits for the pilot gate below, because the deletion, restore and audit paths that make real data safe do not exist until Stage 3.

Stage 2
2 weeks

Hebrew hardening and measurement

Prefix-clitic expansion, a staff-editable acronym gazetteer, name and transliteration variants, the custom Hebrew text-search configuration with a stopword list, and the no-lexical-overlap probe wired in as a build-failing assertion.

Proves: that the sophisticated version beats the simple one by a measured margin — on the synthetic corpus and the public Hebrew retrieval set, using paired per-query tests, because at this sample size mean scores prove nothing. Anything that does not earn its place here does not ship. Confirmation against MAOZ's own corpus waits for the gate below; a synthetic win is a hypothesis, not a result.

Stage 3
2 weeks

Operability

Blue/green rebuilds, daily reconciliation and the erasure path, append-only audit log, rate limits, a heartbeat dead-man's switch, a freshness banner in the UI, and lifecycle alerts on certificates and end-of-life dates.

Proves: that failures become observable and recoverable: a frozen sync, widened permission set or expired certificate makes a noise rather than degrading quietly.

Gate
Real member data enters here — not before

Real-data pilot gate

Everything above runs on synthetic profiles. Before a single real member record is extracted, MAOZ must own the approved field manifest and purpose, name the operations and privacy owners, complete the applicable privacy and security classification, and have the deletion, restore and audit paths built in Stage 3 and tested.

Why here: the safeguards that make real data safe are Stage 3 deliverables, so any earlier stage claiming to prove itself on real profiles would be introducing them before the controls exist. Part A.3 states the same boundary, and the two must not disagree.

Gated on Stage 0 and the pilot gate

Brokered introductions

Broker queue with staff assignment, a match dossier written back to Salesforce on a separate write-scoped credential, and the split into staff-full and member-visible projections.

Proves: nothing technical. This phase is gated on a consent project and on per-sector coverage parity, not on engineering readiness — and opening search to members may itself change MAOZ's regulatory obligations.

04What I don't know yet

Every one of these traces back to a question from Part A.1. Listing them is not hedging — an architecture that claims no open questions is one that hasn't been examined.

ASKED

I put six of these to MAOZ by email during the assignment, each with the assumption I would proceed on if it went unanswered. Three came back. The system is in Hebrew — which retired the bilingual indexing pipeline described in earlier drafts and is the single largest change to this design. Use standard Salesforce object and field names, adding dedicated fields where needed — confirming the assumed data model. Up to thirty minutes to present, with no page limit but a request to write “focused and to the point” — which is why the full component inventory sits in Part A.3 and the appendix rather than here. Three questions were not answered: real example searches, whether v1 is staff-facing or member-facing, and whether MAOZ will run the code themselves. Those remain assumptions, and they are listed below as such.

Q1 · Data

Per-sector coverage, not overall sparseness, is the real risk. If Arab and Haredi members have systematically thinner profiles, the tool under-surfaces exactly the communities MAOZ exists to bridge. I have built the instrument to detect this and made it a launch gate — but not a mechanism to fix it.

And my own fixtures quietly assume the optimistic answer. Every narrative field is populated on every synthetic profile, at near-uniform length — Experience__c runs 66–92 characters across all eighteen. Real CRM data is not like that: partial fill, extreme length variance and entries nobody has touched in three years are the normal state. So Part B measures retrieval on a tidier corpus than a pilot would meet, and the first thing I would ask for is per-field, per-sector fill rates. If they are thin, this is a data project before it is a search project — the exact fork Question 1 was written to expose, now closed against my own evidence rather than left hanging.

Q2 · Consent

Whether existing consent covers “indexed for semantic discovery” is a documents question, not an architecture question. Existing notices and consent may not cover semantic discovery; MAOZ and counsel must verify the actual documents before a real-data pilot.

Q3 · Product

A staff member on WhatsApp may simply be a better product. Today a member gets one name, a warm introduction and a sentence of context within hours. No amount of retrieval quality closes that gap — which is why Stage 0 exists, and why the unit of delivery is the introduction rather than the list.

Q4 · Evidence

Most tuning decisions here are defensible defaults, not validated results. The confidence threshold, the per-intent margin and aspect aggregation stay unfalsifiable until a MAOZ-owned golden set exists. The two knobs this originally named — fusion weights and rerank depth — no longer exist, because measurement removed the components they belonged to. If the ten painful searches never arrive, the first deliverable is a two-week query log, not a model.

Q5 · Fairness

Sector diversification is itself an inference channel. Probing which results shift can reveal which bucket a member competes in. MAOZ's sector labels may proxy or correlate with ethnic origin, political opinion or religious practice. Randomised tie-breaking can reduce, not eliminate, a patient adversary's inference.

Build vs buy

Part of my case against the platform-native option rests on unknowns rather than proven failure. If Salesforce confirms its search index is backed by a Hebrew-capable multilingual model running in-region, a core plank of my argument collapses and the native option becomes materially more attractive. That is a question to put to their account team in writing — and I would revisit the recommendation on a favourable answer rather than defend it out of consistency.

On the shape of this document. The full design names roughly thirty components and around forty data flows; what is shown here is the spine. The concrete threat/control register, verification evidence, residual risks and privacy lifecycle are in Part A.3 — Information security and authorization; no separate appendix or operational runbook is claimed as a deliverable.

Information security is treated as a first-class concern rather than a section at the end: the redaction boundary, the permission predicate inside the scan, per-request Salesforce revalidation, and the rule that no hosted or general-purpose instruction-following LLM receives profile text are architectural choices, not controls added afterwards. The linked Part A.3 develops them in full.

On the claims in this document. Every load-bearing technical assertion here — model licences and context limits, Postgres and pgvector behaviour, the cross-lingual retrieval literature, Salesforce API semantics and the Israeli privacy regime — was checked against primary sources before being written down. Three claims did not survive that check and are corrected above rather than quietly dropped: the argument that Hebrew forces self-hosting, the description of language bias as a single embedding “direction”, and the latency of exact vector search inside Postgres. Figures I could not verify were removed rather than softened.

Part B fed evidence back into the design. A fully dynamic 8-bit export changed retrieval materially and was replaced by a higher-fidelity weight-only export; the proposed reranker worsened the flagship result and was omitted; and a versioned, staff-owned concept vocabulary was added as a visible bridge rather than hiding query-specific rules in code. These are local findings on synthetic fixtures, reproducible with scripts/evaluate.py, not claims of general model quality.
